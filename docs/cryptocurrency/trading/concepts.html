Basic Rules

1. How much can I afford to lose?
Don't invest more than you can afford to lose.
2. Minimum 1:2 risk/reward ratio.
Forex (Major Pairs) 1:2 minimum, ideally 1:2.5
Cryptocurrencies (Crypto) 1:3 or higher
Equities (Stocks) 1:3 or higher
Commodities (e.g., Gold, Oil) 1:3 or higher
3. Maximum 2% of capital may be invested into a position (position size).
For instance, if you have a $1,000 account and you limit your risk to 2%, you are only risking $20 on any particular trade.

Risk/Reward

On a $1,000 account, even with only 4 wins out of 10 trades (a 40% success rate), winning $40 on successful trades (TP) and losing $20 on unsuccessful ones (SL) still results in a $40 profit (4%), since 4 * $40 = $160 > 6 * $20 = $120.

Futures

A derivative contract to buy or sell an asset at a predetermined price on a future date.

Margin

Borrowing money from a broker to trade assets, secured by a deposit known as margin.

Example: Suppose you have $1,000 in your account, and the current price of Bitcoin is 50,000 USD/BTC. Let's say you purchase 0.02 BTC.

Position Size: The position size represents the total value of the trade. In this case, it is calculated as:
Position Size = 0.02 BTC * 50,000 USD/BTC = $1,000.

Margin (deposit): When using leverage, you do not need to commit the full $1,000 of your own capital. Suppose you choose a 10:1 leverage. Margin is the small amount that is locked from your account to secure your leveraged position. In this case, the margin is calculated as:
Margin = Position Size / Leverage = $1,000 / 10 = $100.

This indicates that to open the trade, your broker will reserve $100 from your account as deposit. With this margin, you are able to control a $1,000 position even though you are only using $100 of your own funds. Consequently, the maximum potential loss will be limited to that $100 margin (not your full $1,000 balance).

In general, to avoid a margin call (liquidation of your position), it is advisable to maintain an account balance where the maximum risk (defined by the stop loss) amounts to only a small percentage of your account (for example, 1–2%). Trading with a small account balance (e.g., $1) using leverage is extremely risky, because even a slight price movement can immediately empty the margin.

Capital: $1,000
Risk (%): 2%
Entry Price: $50,000 (Price of 1 BTC)
Quantity purchased: $1,000 / $50,000 = 0.02 BTC
Risk Amount: 2% * $1,000 = $20
Stop Loss: Entry Price − (Risk Amount / BTC quantity) = $50,000 − ($20 / 0.02) = $49,000
If the price of BTC drops to $49,000, then the value of 0.02 BTC will be $980, which results in a $20 loss deducted from the original capital.
Note: The stop loss is based on the market price, not the total capital.

Leverage (tőkeáttétel)

Leverage is the ratio of borrowed money to your own capital. For example, if you use 1:10 leverage, you use 10 units of borrowed money for every 1 unit of your own capital.

Arbitrage

An asset is bought in one market (or exchange) and sold in another for a higher price.

PIP / LOT

In the forex market, trading is measured in LOTs rather than by units used for stocks or cryptocurrencies. If you use 1:5 leverage in the crypto market, you might consider using around 1:20 in the forex market. See: Forex PIP Spreadsheet (tradeonthemarket)
